Stage 01 · Real World
Dissolving depends on particle interaction.
A substance dissolves when its particles separate and are surrounded by water molecules. If particle-particle forces are stronger than particle-water attraction, it settles undissolved as sediment.
🧂 Salt dissolves because Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are pulled apart by polar water molecules.
🏖️ Sand stays solid — Si-O covalent bonds are far too strong for water to break.
🌡️ Warmer water generally dissolves more solid solute — faster kinetics, higher saturation.

What we are doing: Adding solute to water and measuring how much dissolves.
Our aim: Find what controls how much solute dissolves (concentration).
What we will find: Concentration depends on solubility limit, mass, and volume.
🥤
Think of dissolving sugar in tea. Add a little — it dissolves completely. Keep adding — eventually no more dissolves and crystals remain. That limit is the solubility of sugar in hot water.
The Formula
CONCENTRATION OF SOLUTION
C = m_dissolved / V
C = concentration (mol/L)m = mass dissolved (g)V = volume of water (mL)
🌡️
At 20°C, 359 g of NaCl dissolves per litre of water. At 100°C it rises to only ~391 g — salt solubility changes little with temperature, unlike most ionic solids.

🧂 Soluble vs Insoluble
Salt dissolves because water molecules pull Na⁺ and Cl⁻ apart. Sand doesn't — its Si-O bonds are too strong. "Like dissolves like" is the rule!
⚖️ Saturation is the limit
Every solvent can only hold so much solute at a given temperature. Beyond that limit, no more dissolves — the solution is saturated and excess sinks as sediment.
