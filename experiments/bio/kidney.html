Stage 01 · Real World
Kidneys clean the blood.
Blood is filtered in the kidneys, where useful substances are retained and waste is removed as urine. Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons — tiny filtration units that process 180 litres of blood every day.
🩸 Kidneys filter all your blood roughly every 30 minutes.
💧 Only about 1–2 litres of urine is excreted per day — most is reabsorbed.
⚖️ Kidneys regulate blood pressure, pH, and electrolyte balance continuously.

🔬 This Experiment
What we are doing: Studying how kidneys filter blood and produce urine.
Our aim: Find out what controls the filtration rate in the nephron.
What we will find: A formula that predicts the filtration rate from blood flow and pressure.
🚿
Think of a coffee filter. Pressure pushes liquid through a fine membrane — small molecules pass, large ones stay behind. The kidney's glomerulus does exactly this with blood: water and small solutes are squeezed into the nephron, while blood cells and proteins remain in circulation.
The Formula
GLOMERULAR FILTRATION RATE
F = B × (P / 50) × (R / 100)
F = filtration rate (mL/min)B = blood flow (mL/min)P = pressure (mmHg)R = reabsorption (%)
🫘
Your kidneys produce 125 mL of filtrate per minute — but 99% is reabsorbed. Only about 1–2 mL/min actually becomes urine. This is why you produce ~1.5 L of urine despite filtering 180 L of blood daily.
