Stage 01 · Real World
How a football travels through the air.
Every time a player kicks a ball, they unknowingly solve a physics problem. The ball follows a curved path called a parabolic trajectory — governed entirely by launch angle, speed, and gravity.
⚽ A 45° launch angle gives the maximum range on flat ground.
🌍 On the Moon, the same kick would send the ball 6× farther.
💨 Air resistance and spin (Magnus effect) alter the real flight path.

🔬 This Experiment
What we are doing: Launching a projectile and measuring how far and how high it goes.
Our aim: Find what controls the range and maximum height.
What we will find: Formulas that predict the trajectory with high accuracy.
⚽
Think of kicking a football. A shallow kick travels far but stays low. A steep kick goes high but lands nearby. At 45°, you get the perfect balance. Gravity pulls the ball down the same way, every time — only your initial kick determines the path.
Key Formulas
PROJECTILE MOTION EQUATIONS
R = v₀²·sin(2θ) / g
H = v₀²·sin²(θ) / 2g
R = range (m)H = max height (m)v₀ = initial speed (m/s)θ = angle (°)g = gravity (m/s²)
🏆
A ball kicked at 20 m/s at 45° on Earth travels about 40 metres. The same kick on the Moon would land over 240 metres away — well past most football pitches!
